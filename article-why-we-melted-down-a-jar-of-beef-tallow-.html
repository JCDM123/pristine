Why We Melted Down a Jar of Beef Tallow and Never Looked Back

Share

There is a quiet satisfaction in cooking with a fat that has fed families for thousands of years. Beef tallow, the rendered fat from grass-fed cattle, disappeared from most kitchens sometime in the mid-twentieth century when seed oils arrived with a great deal of marketing behind them. We brought it back into our home a few years ago, and it has become one of those small decisions that quietly changed a lot.

A Fat That Actually Holds Up to Heat

Most cooking oils are high in polyunsaturated fats, which are chemically fragile. When you heat them past a certain point, they begin to oxidise, and the compounds they release are not something you want in your food or your body. Beef tallow is almost entirely saturated and monounsaturated fat, which makes it remarkably stable at high temperatures. Think of it like the difference between a wax candle and a paper one: one holds its form under heat, the other does not. We render our own at home using slow-cooked organic beef and bones, straining the fat off and pouring it into jars once it cools to that beautiful pale ivory. The flavour it gives roasted vegetables and seared meat is genuinely something a refined seed oil cannot replicate. We have largely moved away from seed oils at home, though we will be honest and say that mayonnaise is one we have not yet solved completely.

Beef tallow's fatty acid profile sits remarkably close to the oils our own skin produces, which may be exactly why skin responds to it so well.

The Same Fat, Two Uses

What surprised us most when we returned to tallow was how naturally it extended beyond the kitchen. Grass-fed beef tallow is rich in fat-soluble vitamins and conjugated linoleic acid, and its fatty acid profile sits very close to the oils our skin produces naturally. My wife started applying a small amount of our homemade tallow balm to her face each evening, and within a few weeks the dry patches she had managed for years simply settled down. We now use a tallow-based body cream as our everyday moisturiser for the whole family, including the kids, because the ingredient list is short enough to read in one breath. The skin is a living organ and it absorbs what you put on it, so we apply the same standard to our body products as we do to our food.

One thing to do today: swap whatever fat you are currently using for roasting vegetables to beef tallow, ideally from a grass-fed source, and notice both the flavour and how well it holds in a hot oven without smoking.
