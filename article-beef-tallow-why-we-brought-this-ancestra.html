Beef Tallow: Why We Brought This Ancestral Fat Back Into Our Kitchen

Share

For a long time, beef tallow sat in the category of things we were told to fear. It was saturated, it was animal-derived, and according to the nutrition guidance that shaped the 1990s, it was somewhere near the top of a pyramid you were supposed to eat the least of. That guidance has shifted considerably, and the fat our great-grandmothers cooked with without a second thought is worth a second look.

The Pyramid Has Turned

The food pyramid most of us grew up with placed fats and red meat at the narrow tip, to be used sparingly, while grains formed the wide, confident base. Today that picture looks very different. Current nutritional thinking places quality meats and vegetables at the centre of a nourishing diet, and the evidence connecting saturated animal fat to heart disease has turned out to be far weaker than the headlines of thirty years ago suggested. A cow is a genuinely remarkable animal in terms of what it offers us: milk, cream, butter, collagen-rich cuts, and rendered fat, each one a whole food that has sustained human populations for thousands of years. Tallow, the rendered fat from beef, is simply the least celebrated of these and probably the most unfairly sidelined.

What Tallow Actually Gives You

In our house, we collect tallow as a natural part of cooking. We slow-cook beef regularly, partly because the long, low heat draws collagen out of the connective tissue into the liquid, and partly because the flavour is genuinely wonderful. Once the cook is done and the liquid has cooled, the fat sits on top and we skim it off. That collected fat is tallow, and rather than discard it, we use it as our primary cooking fat. It is stable at high heat in a way that many seed oils are not, which matters when you are searing or roasting. It carries fat-soluble vitamins, particularly A, D, E and K2, which the body absorbs more readily from animal sources than from plant ones. It also contains butyrate, a short-chain fatty acid that the cells lining your gut use directly as fuel, supporting the kind of gut environment where digestion works as it should. The flavour it lends to roasted vegetables or a pan of eggs is something the kids notice immediately, and not in a way that needs convincing.

One thing to do today: if you have a slow cooker, put a bone-in beef cut on before the morning school run with water, a splash of apple cider vinegar and whatever vegetables you have. When it is done, let it cool and skim the fat from the top. Store it in a glass jar in the fridge and use it in place of your usual cooking oil this week.
